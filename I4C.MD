# Using Satellite Data to Tell the Story of Flood Risk in East African Cities
**25-min Research Paper Presentation | Innovate4Cities 2026, UN-Habitat**
**Session: "Leveraging data for coordinated action: Digital Tools and Multilevel Governance"**
**Presenter: Priscila Opiyo, Mtaa Safi Initiative CBO**

---

## The Throughline

Three cities, three different "jobs" that satellite data does for flood risk — and one shared gap: data that exists but doesn't yet translate into coordinated action across levels of government.

1. **Kisumu / Ahero & the Lake Victoria Basin** — satellite data shows *how much, and how often* (a slow-motion, transboundary disaster)
2. **Nairobi** — satellite data shows *where and who* (exposure mapping in informal settlements)
3. **Dar es Salaam** — satellite + community data shows *what to do about it* (the closest thing East Africa has to a full data-to-investment pipeline)

The talk opens and closes on a personal note — Ahero is home — and uses that as the emotional throughline that makes the research land for a policy audience.

## Section 1 — Opening (0:00–2:00)

**Talking points:**

- One-sentence roadmap: three cities, three different ways satellite data gets used (or doesn't) — Lake Victoria, Nairobi, Dar es Salaam.

## Section 2 — What Satellite Data Can Actually See (2:00–5:00)

Keep this short and accessible — this is the "methods" section but framed for a governance audience, not a remote sensing audience.

**Talking points:**
- Optical imagery (Landsat, Sentinel-2): good for before/after comparisons of water extent, but blocked by cloud cover — a real problem during rainy-season flood events.
- Radar (Sentinel-1 SAR): sees through clouds, so it's the workhorse for mapping flood extent *while it's happening*.
- Satellite altimetry (TOPEX/Poseidon, Jason series, G-REALM): tracks water *levels* over decades — this is how we know Lake Victoria hit a record high.
- Rapid mapping services (UNOSAT, Copernicus Emergency Management Service): turn raw imagery into usable maps within days of a disaster, often the first hard numbers available to responders.
- **Framing line:** "None of what I'm about to show you required new data collection. It's already published, already mapped, already sitting in reports. The question this talk is really asking is: what happens *after* the map is made?"

## Section 3 — Case 1: Lake Victoria, Kisumu & Ahero — "The Slow Flood" (5:00–9:00)

**The data story:**
- Satellite altimetry going back to 1992 shows Lake Victoria reached a record high of 1137.29 m above sea level on 19 May 2021 — about 2.18 m above the long-term reference level.
- NASA's side-by-side Landsat 8 images (May 2013 vs May 2021) of the Winam Gulf show the visible expansion of open water into what was previously farmland and settlement.
- This wasn't a one-off: the April 2020 Nyando floods (420 km², ~13,300 displaced around Ahero) and the 2021 lake highs are part of the same multi-year wet cycle. Budalang'i in Busia County — also on Lake Victoria's shore — has had at least five major floods, the most recent in 2020 displacing over 40,000 people.

**The governance angle (this is the pivot that makes it research, not just reporting):**
- Lake Victoria is shared by Kenya, Uganda and Tanzania. Some of the explanations offered for the 2020–21 highs pointed to outflow regulation at the Owen Falls Dam in Uganda — a transboundary infrastructure decision affecting communities hundreds of kilometres away.
- Kisumu's own governor publicly called on East African Community heads of state to intervene on lake management.
- **The point:** satellite data here doesn't just show a hazard — it shows a hazard whose *cause* sits at a governance scale (regional/transboundary) that doesn't match the scale at which the *impact* is felt (a single sub-county like Nyando).

**Personal beat:** this is where you can briefly note — without overdoing it — that Ahero is where you're from, and that the 420 km² figure on a satellite map is also someone's home, school, farm.

## Section 4 — Case 2: Nairobi — "Mapping Who's in Harm's Way" (9:00–14:00)

**The data story — two flood events, two years apart:**
- **2024:** UNOSAT satellite imagery (Pléiades, captured 1 May 2024) over Nairobi and Machakos County recorded approximately 22 km² of inundated land, 4,246 affected structures, and 94 km of damaged roads from a single event. Nationally, Nairobi County was among the worst-hit, with an estimated 147,061 people affected and close to 21,000 families displaced.
- **2026:** Flash flooding from 6–7 March 2026 sent the Nairobi River and others over their banks. Mombasa Road, Uhuru Highway, and even JKIA operations were disrupted. By early April 2026, the national death toll stood at 112, with Nairobi accounting for a large share of the roughly 71 deaths reported in mid-March, and over 147,000 people affected or displaced.

**The exposure-mapping story:**
- After the 2026 floods, a government mapping exercise under the **Nairobi Rivers Regeneration Programme** identified 37 flood-prone neighbourhoods across the city — clustered along three corridors: the Nairobi River corridor (CBD, Globe, Gikomba, Eastleigh, Industrial Area), the Mathare River corridor (Mathare, Korogocho, Lucky Summer), and the Ngong River corridor (Kibera, South B/C, Mukuru Kwa Reuben, Mukuru Kwa Njenga, Kilimani).
- **The killer fact for this section:** these are *the same neighbourhoods* that appear in flood response reports going back to the catastrophic floods of 1997–98. Climate change is intensifying the rainfall — but the *locations* of risk have been known and mapped, repeatedly, for nearly three decades.

**The governance angle:**
- Satellite-derived exposure maps exist — UNOSAT in 2024, government mapping in 2026. What's harder to find is evidence that this satellite-confirmed exposure data has driven *upstream* decisions: drainage infrastructure investment, riparian land-use enforcement, resettlement planning.
- **The point:** this is a case where the "where" question has been answered repeatedly by data, but the multilevel link from *national/satellite-derived risk maps* to *county-level infrastructure budgets and informal settlement upgrading* is the missing piece — not the data itself.

## Section 5 — Case 3: Dar es Salaam — "From Data to Investment" (14:00–19:00)

This is your strongest "solutions" case study — use it to show what a fuller pipeline looks like.

**The data story:**
- Dar es Salaam is one of the fastest-growing cities in Africa — from roughly 2.5 million people in 2002 to over 5.5 million by the early 2020s, projected to exceed 10 million by 2040. An estimated 70% of residents live in informal settlements, concentrated along the Msimbazi River and its tributaries.
- December 2011–January 2012 floods displaced at least 10,000 people and killed 40.
- **Ramani Huria** ("Open Map" in Swahili), launched in 2015 with funding from the World Bank and implementation by the Humanitarian OpenStreetMap Team, trained university students and community members to map flood-prone areas — combining satellite imagery, drone (UAV) surveys, and household-level flood depth/extent surveys (conducted 2017–2018) into open data on OpenStreetMap.
- A 2025 peer-reviewed study (*Journal of Flood Risk Management*) used a digital terrain model from the World Bank-funded Tanzania Urban Resilience Program — validated against that same Ramani Huria community survey data — to track geomorphological change and displacement of informal settlements along the Msimbazi over time.

**The governance angle — the full pipeline:**
- Community-collected ground data (Ramani Huria) → validated against/combined with satellite-derived terrain and imagery data → feeding into a national resilience programme (Tanzania Urban Resilience Program) → informing the Msimbazi Basin development and resettlement programme (World Bank-financed).
- **The point:** this is the closest East Africa has to a *closed loop* — community data and satellite data validating each other, flowing into a programme with an actual budget line and an implementing institution at city and national level.
- Caveat to include for intellectual honesty: this took roughly a decade (2015 to a 2025 published validation study) — so "closing the loop" is possible, but slow, and it required sustained international financing to get there.

## Section 6 — Synthesis: Three Jobs, One Gap (19:00–22:00)

**Talking points — bring the three cases together explicitly:**

- **Job 1 — "How much, how often" (Lake Victoria / Kisumu):** satellite data reveals a long-term trend and a transboundary cause, but the governance scale needed to act (regional/EAC-level) doesn't match the scale where impact lands (a sub-county).
- **Job 2 — "Where and who" (Nairobi):** satellite data repeatedly confirms exposure in the same settlements over decades, but the link from national risk maps to local infrastructure investment and land-use decisions remains the weak point.
- **Job 3 — "What to do" (Dar es Salaam):** the only case where data — community *and* satellite — actually reached an investment decision, and it took a decade plus dedicated international financing.

**The unifying argument:**
> "Across all three cities, the limiting factor was never the satellite data. Sentinel, Landsat, Pléiades, and altimetry data already exist, already cover these basins, and are often already published. What varies — and what determines whether that data leads anywhere — is whether there's a *governance pathway*: an institution, a budget line, a mandate, that connects the scale at which the data is produced to the scale at which a decision needs to be made."

This is also your natural bridge to the next presentations in the session (South Africa's National Strategic Hub, and the land governance/digital territorial systems talk) — you can explicitly flag that you're setting up the *problem* that those institutional models are designed to solve.

## Section 7 — Recommendations (22:00–24:00)

Frame these as what "digital tools and multilevel governance" needs to mean in practice for flood risk:

1. **Regional data-sharing for transboundary basins** — Lake Victoria needs an EAC/Lake Victoria Basin Commission-level mechanism for sharing satellite-derived water level and inundation data *and* for linking that data to upstream infrastructure decisions (e.g., dam outflow management).
2. **Embed satellite-derived exposure data into standing governance structures** — not one-off post-disaster maps, but recurring inputs into city and national planning bodies (the kind of "single source of truth" model that data hubs like South Africa's National Strategic Hub represent).
3. **Treat community-generated data as infrastructure, not a pilot** — Ramani Huria shows community mapping can validate and ground-truth satellite data, but it needs sustained institutional ownership, not project-cycle funding.
4. **Tie data products to budget lines** — a flood map only changes outcomes if it's wired into a capital investment or resettlement programme with a named institutional owner, as in the Msimbazi case.
